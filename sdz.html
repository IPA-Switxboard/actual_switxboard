Worldwide, urbanization is predicted to continue and grow, resulting in more informal settlements in and around big cities. Informality poses economic, but also social and other risks. Informality, however, is mostly a regulatory artifact: sub-optimal regulations actually create the informal economy. Most of the activity that takes place informally like informal settlements, informal employment, informal businesses etc., is a natural response to regulatory barriers that prevent low-income groups from legally building homes, working, or operating businesses. Against this background, SDZs as developed by the SDZ Alliance are a program to localize the UN SDGs in and around big cities. The primary mechanism for achieving this is to introduce a special legal framework and governance structure to a limited geographical area. As such, an SDZs is a type of Special Economic Zone (SEZ), but with a distinct social and SDGs profile, combining brownfield with greenfield development. It introduces innovative practices for documentation, access to finance (“bankability”), land use, development, business regulation

  • in particular for starting and operating MSMEs - and public services,
    designed to lower the barriers between the informal and formal sectors.
    This will lead to a win-win situation for all groups including the municipality,
    the region, and the national government.

    public administration, aligned with UN Good Governance and UN Global Compact

What SDZ

An SDZ in Ethiopia would be called “SME City”

to signify the importance of facilitating life for businesses and creating jobs in the formal economy, fully in line with Ethiopia’s Ten Years Perspective Development Plan (2021-2030).

The selection of the location of an SDZ/SME City

would be based on suitability criteria as explained in the pre-feasibility study. An optimal site should be determined by proximity to infrastructure and labor, availability of land, legal and political considerations, and other factors.

A social enterprise company (“SDZ Developer”)

would be formed involving investors, residents and the municipality. The SDZ Developer for the SME City would adopt a blended-finance approach including development financing with grants and concessional loans as well as private financing with Corporate Social-Responsibility (CSR), social-impact and market-return-on-investment layers, meeting ESG criteria. In addition to the project proponents, government entities at the national, regional, and municipal levels, and SDZ residents would have equity stakes in the SDZ Developer.

The SDZ Developer would purchase the land lease from the government

develop a masterplan and cadaster, and provide for infrastructure and basic services by contracting with third parties which can of course include traditional public and private providers to avoid unnecessary disruptions. The SDZ would regulate activities within the zone and formalize the tenure of existing informal residents and informal businesses by offering registration, subleases, and business licenses subject to standards that are conducive to low-income communities.

Formal sector residents and enterprises of the area

would benefit from an administration that overcomes the barriers to living, working, and operating successfully. Moreover, such residents would be shareholders in the SDZ Developer, entitling them to a share of its profit. Informal and low-income sector residents and small businesses would benefit from formal documentation, legal protection, access to finance (“bankability”) and a greater market as well as from social services. Services would include education, training and skill development, including in new technologies
